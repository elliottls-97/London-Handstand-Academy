How the press works

Most people train the press as a single explosive effort and wonder why it never lands. The truth is the press is a sequence, and the sequence has an order. Your feet, shoulders and hips have to work together as one connected chain — if any link is weak or fires out of order, you either get stuck on the floor or planche your way up and lose the shape.

01
Stage one · the start of the press

Take-Off

The take-off is where the press begins, and almost every fault shows up here in one of two ways. Either you lean too far forward, which points to a lack of flexibility, or you collapse, which means you don't yet have the strength to hold the position.

To take off properly the scapula needs to be elevated — push tall and create space through the shoulders before anything leaves the floor. Engage the hip flexors to pull the knees down, and let the lower back draw the hips into anterior pelvic tilt.

The key mistake

Slip into posterior pelvic tilt and you'll end up in a tuck planche — far too much pressure through the base of the palm.

Tuck Handstand

The scapula needs to be elevated to properly take off, and the tuck handstand is the best teacher there is — it shows you how to move the hips and elevate the scapula at the same time.

Because the centre of mass is much lower, it's easier to balance, but you'll feel it far more in the shoulders. That's the point.

  • Engage the hip flexors to pull the knees down.
  • Let the lower back draw the hips into anterior pelvic tilt.
  • Push tall — actively elevate the scapula.
Mistake to avoid

Don't drop into posterior pelvic tilt — you'll end up in a tuck planche with far too much pressure through the base of the palm.

Stage two · the engine of the press

Compression

Compression is where the press is won or lost. The feet, shoulders and hips have to work together as one chain — connected, not three separate efforts. When the chain is whole, the press feels light. When a link drops, everything else overworks to compensate.

Lower back engagement is essential — it's the anchor of the whole pattern. Without it you can't hold the shape under load, and you certainly can't reverse it cleanly.

03
Stage three · the reversal to vertical

Unrolling

Unrolling is the controlled reversal that takes you from compressed shape to vertical. The ability to unroll is built on the lower back engagement from stage two — without that anchor, the unroll has nothing to work against.

Watch for this

If you lean too far forward, you'll try to compensate — and end up with a rounded back. That's exactly what we want to avoid.

Bringing it together · hero exercise

Back to Wall Press

The back-to-wall press teaches the shape and the compression with the wall taking some of the load — so you can focus on engaging the right things in the right order.

★ Hero exercise

Back to Wall Press

  • Make sure your full upper back and head are on the wall — this takes more of the weight.
  • With the wall supporting you, focus on engaging the hip flexors.
  • Keep the back rounded throughout.
If it's heavy on the wrists

Turn the hands out to the side — it eases the load through the wrists so you can keep training.

The ultimate drill · all three stages in one

Chest to Wall Press

This is the one that ties everything together. The chest-to-wall press trains all three stages — take-off, compression and unrolling — in a single movement, which is exactly why it's the centrepiece. Your feet can't go anywhere, so your shoulders can't either: you either compress and unroll correctly, or the wall rejects you.
